Exploring the relationship between Salary Gender, Age, Education Level, Experience and Role

For our project, we wanted to study factors that can affect a salary. This information is relevant to those in the Data Analytics Bootcamp because most of us will apply for new jobs once the boot camp ends. Knowing which job titles offer better pay will be helpful during the job search. We also can use our insights to negotiate a higher salary during the hiring process.

We have all heard of the gender wage gap and were interested in seeing how true that is for this data set. We also assumed that salary would increase as age, education level, and years of experience increase, but we wanted to ensure that these assumptions were correct.

Note: In the sourced dataset, sample responses for "Gender" were limited to "Male" or "Female". As gender selection options expand in the workforce, our hope is that the relevant data becomes more widely available.

Analysis

When looking at lowest to highest salaries, no matter the gender, jobs with the word “junior” or “associate” in the title tend to have lower salaries, jobs with the word “senior” or “manager” have salaries in the middle, and jobs with the word “director” have the highest salaries.

For both genders, the highest salary was for “Director of Data Science,” which is good news for us, as we are entering the field of data analytics.

Analysis

When comparing salaries to age, it is clear that salary tends to increase as age increases.

This correlation is unsurprising because with age comes more experience and promotion opportunities.

In addition, salaries can increase from less than $40k at age 22 to more than $180k at age 60 - a whopping $140k increase throughout a career.

Analysis

As expected, salary tends to increase as years of experience increases.

However, one interesting insight is that after 20 years of experience, salaries appear to plateau and stay approximately the same until 35 years of experience.

Analysis

Our research shows a strong positive correlation between education and salary.

Interestingly, the wage gap for women decreases as they earn higher education degrees.

It is important to note that the correlation between education levels and salary can vary across industries, job sectors, and geographic locations. Other factors such as experience, job performance, demand for specific skills, and market conditions also influence salary levels.

Analysis

For roles where there were correlated "Junior" and "Senior" positions, Software Engineer saw the greatest increase percentage for both Women and Men at 210% and 215% increase respectively.

Women working in the role of Project Manager saw a substantial increase from Junior to Senior relative to their male counterparts at 208%. Additionally, for Women, 4 of 7 roles sampled saw significantly greater increase in percentage relative to the Junior role.

When analyzing Salary Increase Amount, there was greater disparity between genders. For example, Female Marketing Coordinators received a $55,000 increase vs $30,000 for their male counterparts. For Software Engineers, Men received an average of an $110,000 increase vs $98,0000 for Women.
